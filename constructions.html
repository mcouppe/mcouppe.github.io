TECHNICAL DESCRIPTION

camera : Nikon D3200 , Nikon D810
lens : Nikon 70 mm, Nikon 24-105mm
place : Ivry-sur-Seine (94), France
date : November 2014 - March 2015

During my studies in Photography, my school started a partnership with the city where the school was located, Ivry-Sur-Seine. The city was in the middle of an intensive change of the urban plan and policy . This city used to be the typical french working class neighboorhood. A lot of brick houses housing workers and big factories in all types of industry.In 2010, the city launched the first modernising urban plan for years. A lot of industrials zones had been left to dust and even if the city stayed under the power of a communist mayor, the inhabitants gained more and more buying power and wanted more shops and a better-looking neighbourhood. With this urban plan, a lot of well-known buildings were destroyed as the Cité Gagarine and many residents had to fled to cheaper places, while new people from higher social background moved in.
In 2014, when we started the project, the gap between financially stables and insecures was huge. All those new investments into urban plans came with a kind of poverty hunt and a lot of communities were targeted.
At this time, there was 3 huge Romani and Gypsy campsites on deserted fields aside the rails of old freight lines, and a few more of little ones around the city. The most important one was called the "Camp Truillot" and around 400 people lived there. The press called it "le plus grand bidonville du Val-de-Marne", the biggest slum of the department.
It's in this campsite I worked for several months, looking for "photographics words" to express as I could the hard life the inhabitants were living. I was supported all along with a romanian classmate who helped me a lot to communicate with the people, to traduce and mostly to be accepted as a photographer.

My first times in Truillot Street were without any camera. We tried to see what was going on behind the fences. How can we talk about this people and their hard reality with our cameras ?
We spent a few weeks talking, sharing, expressing ourselves with the residents. We learned to know eachothers. Very soon, we were seem as regulars in the camp. Some families welcomed us in their homes for hours to talk and share syrup recipes.
The news of our repeated come and go spread fast, with good and bad aspects. Some residents looked us as a threat because a lot of press photographers and journalists had worked with the city hall and alt-right to despise them publicly with racial slurs among others xenophobics obsenities. So a lot of photographers had came here to stole pictures of them, sneaking around the camp and questioning the children. This had been a treason for the inhabitants and they wanted to protect themselves from this because people started to attack the camp at night, setting fires, writing death threats on the streets wall.
With this background, we asked some residents how we could help them as photographer. We weren't press photographer, we couldn't change the mind of xenophobics but we still wanted to be involved in their environment with photography. And some of them started to ask us to take photographs of their families. A lot of families didn't have any printed picture of them or of their children, and almost no one had any recent ones.
That was our open door to help and be definitively accepted there. We organised regulars photoshoot, almost 3 per week, and every week, we distributed the pictures we took, edited and printed, ready to be framed. A new relationship with photography initiated in Truillot Street. Soon, the children became creatives about it and we learned them how to take good pictures, how to think a photograph as a game.

For me, it became clear the harder part of the life here was the constructions. It was the sore spot of the everyday life. Even if the "Camp Truillot" was organised as a proper town with cafés, specifics communities neighbourhood and even an High Street, the bad weather, the malicious behaviours of the neighbours and the police coming regulary to destroy a few houses : the comfort was really frail and scarce. The whole camp was built on a mud field so even the strongest house had to be restored and rebuilt every month.
During the winter, we witnessed a dozen of intense floods. Those floodings made moving around the camp a perilous adventure, destroying a lot of houses. And always, after the floods came the rats. The ground, teared apart by the water, was then full of tunnels were the animals proliferated, moving the fundations of constructions. The major work was to elevate the floor of houses and building complex systems of retractables stairs so the rats won't come inside.
An other issue of such insecure constructions was the isolation from the cold. The only way to make some heat inside was by using artisanal stoves releasing a very toxic emission of black smoke. And most of all, those stove were increasing the risk of fire during night, wich was the nightmare of the parents living with children and elderly.The risk was already quite high because of the poor electrical system they shared.
All these issues made me realize the bad housing was their curse. It was destroying everything from their souvenirs to their clothe, and even their health. Some of the teenagers, going to the city schools told us they could never "fit" because of the place they lived. That's why I choose to document those frail constructions. To leave a trace of what kind of reality children and teenager have to cop with even in the XXIst century.
The "Camp Truillot" had been evacuated by police forces in July 2015. We stayed with its inhabitants until the last moment. A few of them had been deplaced to "social housing", mostly families, but the larger part of the people fled to other camp all around the parisian suburb.
